what I actually believe

Most AI products being built right now are ChatGPT wrappers with a coat of paint. A text box, a system prompt, a branded loading spinner. That's not AI engineering. That's prompt formatting.

Real AI engineering means thinking about orchestration — how multiple models or agents collaborate, pass context, resolve disagreements. It means thinking about failure modes: what happens when the model is confidently wrong, when agents contradict each other, when memory retrieval returns something stale.

That's the part most people skip. I don't skip it. Skipping it means I'd be shipping something I don't understand, and I can't do that.

The 'why' is what I'm actually building toward. The output can be perfect and it still feels unfinished if I don't know why it worked.

on the AI wave

People don't realize how big this is. There has never been a technological advancement that moves at this pace — where you wake up every day and something significant has shifted. Anthropic ships something. Meta releases a model. A paper drops that makes last month's SOTA look outdated.

This has never happened before. Not like this.

And the people who will survive it — not just survive, but move with it — are the ones who understand what's actually happening inside these systems. Not as a user. Not as someone who prompts well. As someone who knows why attention works, why transformers scale, what's actually happening in the forward pass.

That's why I'm building an LLM from scratch. Not to reinvent anything. Just so I can never be surprised by it.

what I won't build

A technically impressive system that nobody needs is just an expensive experiment. If I can't picture the person who's going to use it — specifically, not abstractly — I lose motivation fast.

I'm not interested in demo culture. A system that works in a 3-minute screen recording and breaks in production is not a system. It's a video.

The things that keep me going are the problems that don't have clean solutions. The ones where the engineering challenge and the user problem are the same problem.

the hardest thing I've solved

The challenge in CodeSensei wasn't building 5 agents. Any engineer can spin up 5 LLM calls. The hard part was making them produce one coherent, non-contradictory output when they share zero state and know nothing about each other.

Early versions had the risk agent flagging files the structure agent had already dismissed as irrelevant. The impact agent contradicted the execution agent's dependency map. The synthesizer would merge these and produce analysis that was internally inconsistent — worse than a single agent would have done alone.

The fix was treating conflict as a first-class concept. Not merging outputs. Not averaging them. Building a synthesizer that detects disagreements, classifies them by type, and resolves them with a weighted priority system based on agent role and evidence confidence.

5,000 to 10,000 lines. Under 90 seconds. Sub-10-second query responses. That number took weeks to get to.

[agent:risk] [CONFLICT] flagging auth.py — contradicts structure agent ruling
[synthesizer] [RESOLVE] conflict type: scope_mismatch — applying priority weight
[synthesizer] [OK] resolved — risk agent deferred, structure ruling preserved
[pipeline] [OK] 5 agents merged → 1 coherent analysis · 87s · 0 contradictions
