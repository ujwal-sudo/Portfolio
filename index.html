// about

I build first.
Theorize later.

Working code teaches me things no paper ever could. I'll hack something together fast, get it running, then obsess over every layer of why it actually worked.

That's how CodeSensei was born — a rough multi-agent system in days, then weeks understanding why the synthesizer was failing, why agents contradicted each other, why parallel execution introduced race conditions I hadn't anticipated.

What I'm chasing is making something genuinely complex feel effortless to the person using it. Five agents analyzing 10,000 lines simultaneously — none of that should be visible. They type a question, they get an answer. The complexity is my problem, not theirs.

current obsession
Building an LLM from scratch. Not fine-tuning, not APIs. Every transformer block, every attention head, every gradient update. Most people use LLMs as black boxes. I want to know what's happening inside the forward pass.
belief
AI is the biggest advancement in technology that has ever happened — and the rate of change is accelerating daily. The only way to not get swept away is to understand it from within. No shortcuts. No wrappers. No skipping the hard parts.
engineering philosophy
Can't ship something I don't fully understand. The output can be perfect — doesn't matter. The 'why' is what I'm actually building toward. Most AI products are ChatGPT wrappers. Real engineering means thinking about orchestration, failure modes, agent communication.
